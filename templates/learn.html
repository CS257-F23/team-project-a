What is the Goldilocks Zone?

Life, as we know it, requires liquid water. And for water on a planet to remain liquid, that planet has to reside within a certain distance from the star it's orbiting. If it's too close, all water on it boils. If it's too far, all water freezes. Thus, the distance has to be just right- and that's why scientists have dubbed the range of such distances the "Goldilocks Zone."

How did we determine the Goldilocks Zone?

Scientists disagree on the size of the Goldilocks Zone, even for our own solar system. Thus, many ways of calculating whether a planet is in it have emerged. Our website uses the Solar Equivalent AU equation, which works by comparing a given star to our own sun. The equations we used are from bbvaopenmind.com and planetarybiology.com, and essentially the idea is to assume that since the Earth is in the Sun's goldilocks zone, and we know where the sun's habitable zone begins and ends, we can approximate the habitable zone of any other star using it's absolute stellar luminosity, which is a measurement of how much energy that star gives off per second. If we compare this to the sun's stellar luminosity, we can use our data for how big the sun's goldilocks zone is to scale the goldilocks zone of that star. Then, to determine if a planet is in that sun's goldilocks zone, we just use the semi-major axis, which is a measurement of how close that planet orbits to it's star, and see if it is in the star's goldilocks zone.
